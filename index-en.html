How therapy could help

Therapy primarily helps to achieve a genuine understanding and acceptance of oneself. Unlike temporary fixes, this process brings lasting positive results. It increases confidence and happiness and empowers people to live fully, true to their own values.

If we both decide to work together, during therapy sessions we will talk about your thoughts, fears, worries and feelings and whatever else burdens you. I will listen to you carefully. I will not judge, nor give labels, nor give you a diagnosis. I will not give advice.

My goal is instead to empathically understand where you are coming from. When your openness is met with empathy and acceptance, it will enable you to work through any unresolved issues and to regain trust in yourself and in life. Confidentiality is guaranteed.

We think that we listen but rarely do we listen, with real understanding, true empathy. Yet listening, of this very special kind, is one of them most potent forces for change I know.

Carl Ranson Rogers

Person Centered Approach

In therapy sessions with me you will experience the Person Centered Approach (PCA). This approach belongs to humanistic psychology and was developed in the USA in the middle of the 20th century by the psychotherapist Carl R. Rogers.

In those days it was revolutionary because Rogers as the psychotherapist refused to play the expert with regard to the client’s life. Instead he tried to like and understand his client, therefore the name ‘person-centered’. Rogers saw that clients are the best experts when it comes to their own lives.

The theory behind PCA, in brief, proposes that every living organism has a built-in programme of growth. In an ideal environment the organism develops both physically and mentally in an optimal way. If for any reason this programme is disturbed and the natural growth is stunted, it leads to disturbances.

The goal of psychotherapy is to provide a physically and psychologically safe environment where clients can face their problems honestly, without hiding behind a social facade.

Rogers realised that if the psychotherapist is able to create an environment in which clients and their issues are unconditionally accepted, while at the same time staying empathic and authentic (the psychotherapist himself is not hiding behind a facade), the result is a spontaneous ‘correction’ of the programme.

Therapy effectively deals with:

  • problems in relationships
  • lack of direction in life
  • addictions
  • inability to say no
  • low self-esteem
  • complexes/anxieties
  • procrastination
  • fear of authorities
  • stress and burnout
  • loneliness
  • fear of death
  • feelings of guilt
  • problems with sleep
  • traumas and life crises

According to your wishes therapy also focus on personal growth, interrelationships and communication skills.

Why I became a therapist

I was born in 1983 and grew up in Bratislava, Slovakia. I later lived in Vienna, where I studied first Informatics and then Psychology. Now I live and work in Prague.

I experienced a hugely positive shift in my life thanks to therapy. Up until my early thirties I was quite successfully developing my IT career. Then I went through a life crisis because of a break-up, which forced me to seek answers. In the end it was therapy that transformed my perception of life. Thanks to it I realised that life was fine as it was. The beauty of this insight was that it was a real experience and not just mere words.

At this point I found my new calling. I started to study psychology and to train in the same kind of psychotherapy as I had benefited from: the person centered approach.

I left university after three semesters. I felt that more theory would not make me a more empathic—and therefore better—therapist. I then continued solely with my training, which I finished in 2019.

What I value about therapy

Therapy is both practical and transformative. It has radically changed my perception of life for the better and I find it rewarding to work with people on regaining their trust in this miracle called life.
